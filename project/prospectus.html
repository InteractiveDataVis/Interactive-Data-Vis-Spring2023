Project Prospectus

After proposing an initial project related to the proximity of water to economic and social activity, and finding that potentially difficult to source in census data, here are new notes for a prospectus about Utah.

I did start compiling information about Guam as an interesting case study. However, census data is more limited for this US territory, and given the semester timeline, I didn't feel I'd do justice to the island.

Phase 1: Ideate

I don't know that much about Utah, and have only been there once to visit friends in Salt Lake City. But Utah has been the fastest growing state over the last decade. While it may be easy to explain away the state's growth with stereotypes about Moron family size , I want to explore addtional reasons for population growth in this dynamic state.

Internal migration

There may be a story about internal migration within the US to explain population growth in Utah.

A diversifying population

While Utah is one of the whiter states, the 2020 census, nearly a quarter of the population is non-white. In Salt Lake City, the most populous part of Utah, the percentage of residents who are non-white went from 26% in 2010 to 32.4% in 2020.

Migration to cities

Perhaps we can see the general trend of migration from rural to urban as operating in Utah.

Economic opportunities

Utah's flat corporate tax could make it more appealing for small business development

Other potential themes

  • Housing growth at a time of national stagnation
  • Proximity to natural recreation
  • Public safety
  • Role of the church in civil society and social programs
  • Migration from Mexico

Phase 2: Develop

The approach of the project would mostly be exploratory. I expect that the following approach will be made to try to contextualize Utah's population growth.

  1. Comparisons could be made between the state and others that have experienced growth over the same period of time. This could help explain larger trends for which Utah is the most concentrated version
  2. Comparisons between Utah and the states shrinking the most in population. This approach could contrast policy valences, and attempt to pull together some interesting correlations with the approach above.
  3. Do time series analysis of census data for the state, which could demonstrate the rate of growth in recent decades.
  4. Create a map that explains population changes over time at a more granular level than the state itself.
  5. Simple research behind potential causes for population growth and related data sets could be include to enhance census data.
