Visualizing Utah's Population Growth

This page visualizes Utah's growth relatively to other states' growth rates between 2010 - 2019 based off of census data. It also illustrates the originating nations of migrants to states of interest (US vs. Abroad), and a breakdown of US states from which migrants are moving to Utah during the time period of the 2020 US Census Results.

Note: visualizations are best viewed in a browser window of 1200px width or equivalent on high density screen. Not well validated on mobile devices like touch tablets or mobile phones.

Which US states grew fastest and slowest?

To put Utah's growth over 2010 - 2019 in perspective, it has both the highest growth rate and the highest population increase of the top five fastest growing states (Idaho, Texas, North Dakota and Neveda). The rate of growth of these states is contrasted by the Pennsylvania, the slowest growing states of the 2020 census, and three shrinking states with a negative growth rate.

In the graph below, you can hover of the bars to get both the growth rate and population change for each of the states of interest. At risk of stating the obvious, having a higher growth rate does not mean that other states with lower rates had a smaller population growth in terms of population size; see Texas and Pennsylvania for examples.

Migration from abroad v. other states

With general perceptions about global and US birth rates and migration, it would be interesting to investigate the data for both fast and slow/ negative growth. Are there correlations that could suggestion:

  1. That populations with high rates of growth are fueled by migration from outside the US?
  2. Conversely, are internal migrants from other US states contributing to faster growing states relative increase in size?
  3. Could it be the case that slower or shrinking states have more foreign born migration?
  4. Etc.

Hovering over the stacked bar chart above reveals a muddy picture with which to answers these questions. Of the fastest growing states, Texas shows the most migration from abroad at 28% of migrants, but 20% or more of Pennsylvania and Illinois incoming population is another country. Both fast growing (Idaho, North Dakota) and shrinking states (Mississippi, West Virgina) have 10% or less of their migrant pool coming from outside the US.

Which states are people leaving to settle in Utah?

The chart below is a circle pack, with each circle representing a state. The size of the each circle is proportional to the number of migrants from state to Utah. The graph can be toggled to a color coding by region or division (see Census regions and division in data sources below).

While viewing this chart coding by region, we can see that most new Utahns come from the West, with the South coming in second place. When coding color by division, one of the more interesting findings is that the number of migratns from states to Utah in the West is composed of roughly equal parts larger population states in the Pacific division, and less densely populated Mountain states.
